## Where my head is right now

**Compression dominates the conversation. Expansion is where the value sits.** Nearly all the enterprise AI talk is about [[ai-expands-not-compresses|compression]]: automate the workflow, do it faster, remove a human. That's genuinely useful for the work that can be compressed. But the workflows that need real thinking can't be, and that's where the second mode lives - pointing AI at a growing [[the-context-bottleneck|context vault]] to expand and pressure-test my thinking, lifting the quality of the output rather than the volume. I made this the spine of my half of the first Hotsheet episode with Brian.

**Subscribable brains as a new medium.** Publishing my vault as an MCP endpoint felt different from publishing an article. An article gets read once; a subscribable brain gets queried repeatedly, in contexts I can't predict, by AIs I'll never see. I'm writing for traversal now, not just for reading. It also answers a question I kept circling: can a [[can-a-second-brain-be-shared|second brain be shared]]? Merging two vaults flattens two perspectives into one, and that's where it breaks ([[one-idea-per-note|one idea per note]] assumes one thinker per note). Subscribable brains keep the perspectives discrete. My vault stays mine, yours stays yours, and an AI subscribes to both and traverses across them, finding connections neither of us would make alone. You federate the thinking rather than merging it. I've tested it by subscribing to Brian Madden's MCP alongside my own vault: two brains, one session, perspectives intact.

**The governance problem is closer than people think.** [[the-governance-gap]] keeps getting validated. Every conversation I have with enterprise customers surfaces the same tension: individuals building powerful context layers that their organisations can't see, govern, or retain. [[shadow-ai-follows-the-shadow-it-pattern|Shadow AI]] is already here. Nobody has a good answer yet. Two things sharpened on the podcast. First, the real exposure is aggregation: individually harmless thoughts synthesise into something genuinely company-sensitive. Second, Brian and I have now moved our own second brains into Citrix-governed systems, above board, rather than running them as personal skunkworks. Same lesson as shadow IT - you don't win by blocking, you win by providing a sanctioned path.

**Two halves that might fit together (still chewing on this).** Brian's half of the show was about agents that act inside the same apps and environments humans already use. My half was about context vaults and [[ai-expands-not-compresses|expansion]]. We didn't plan it, but by the end the two halves kept reaching for each other: an agent that knows me through my context vault and can also act inside my actual governed environment is a different proposition to either piece alone. I'm not settled on this. Brian and I discussed it and it gave me something to ponder - is the real unlock the convergence rather than either half on its own?

**It's a practice gap, and it's quietly widening.** The timeline is compressed. Models became genuinely good at this around November 2025, when writable memory turned a chatbot into a [[ai-as-co-thinker|collaborator]], and through Q1 2026 a small group of pioneers independently arrived at the same patterns. Even if capabilities froze today, there's a decade of work left to exploit what's already here. Most people still use AI as a search replacement; a handful have figured out [[ai-as-persistent-collaborator|persistent collaboration]]. The gap between them is a practice gap, not a technology gap, and practice gaps close through imitation, not invention. So the sceptics' question - "is the next model better enough?" - is the wrong one. The right one is "have you fully exploited what's already here?" The adopters are still a tiny share of knowledge workers but disproportionately influential, and [[the-experience-gap|the output gap]] is getting harder to ignore. When colleagues start asking "how did you produce that?" the tipping point isn't far behind. None of this makes me [[ai-makes-thinking-harder-not-easier|10x more productive; it makes me 10x more insightful]], and insight is invisible until it produces something.

**The thesis has matured from a name into a body of work.** The umbrella is [[augmented-cognition|Augmented Cognition]]; the method within it is [[chain-of-cognition|Chain of Cognition]]; the enemy it defeats is [[reheated-thinking|Reheated Thinking]] and the [[meaning-shaped-slop|meaning-shaped slop]] it leaves behind. The clearest way I explain the difference: the same AI is a [[the-freezer-and-the-sous-chef|freezer or a sous-chef]] depending on the posture I take toward it. As a freezer of pre-cooked positions it reheats; as a sous-chef under my direction it helps me cook. The microwave didn't change, the cook did. That is my answer to "but doesn't your method also use AI?" - it's a category, not a technique. I'm building it out a piece a week on davebrear.ai: [[cook-dont-reheat|Cook, Don't Reheat]] named the enemy, [[chain-of-cognition-how-to-cook-instead|Chain of Cognition]] named the method, and [[how-the-sausage-was-made|How the Sausage Was Made]] showed it in action. It is now also airing on the Citrix AI Hotsheet, the podcast I co-host with Brian Madden.
