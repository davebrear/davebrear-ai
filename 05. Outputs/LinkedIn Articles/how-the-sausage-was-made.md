# How the Sausage Was Made: A Worked Example of Thinking with AI

Last Thursday I named the method I use to think and produce outputs with AI. This Thursday, as promised, is the proof of how it works.

Fair warning - this is a niche piece. A director's commentary on last week's article. If the workings of someone's writing process interest you, read on. If not, my next piece returns to broader ground.

If you have not read last week's [[chain-of-cognition-how-to-cook-instead|Chain of Cognition: how to cook instead]] yet, go and read it first for two reasons.

1. **As a primer**: the walk-through below references the method by phase - Phase 1 capture, Phase 2 MOC, Phase 3 draft and edit. Last week's piece is where they are defined.
2. **As a reference for what got made**: I will be pointing at specific sections of that piece throughout. The demonstration carries more weight when you have the output in front of you, because the build below produces the lines you have already read.

The quoted lines below show the verbatim inputs and responses as we shaped the piece. I am **Me**. The AI is **Max**.

My starting position? I want to explain the method for Augmented Cognition in a way that someone can follow. I have nothing on the page, so let's get started walking through the sections of last week's article to see how we fleshed them out.

## Phase 1: capture

Phase 1 starts with capture. The first thing I dictated was the plan:

> **Me >** *Six sections, ~approx 1000 words. 1. Callback to last week 2. The chef's kitchen (sous-chef framing) 3. Naming the method: Chain of Cognition 4. Explain the method 5. The value of human in the loop 6. Coda / next-week tease*

That captured the rough shape of what I thought I was going to write.

Then I asked the AI to surface adjacent atoms and notes from my vault.

> **Me >** *Show me what we have on Chain of Cognition already.*

It surfaced a cluster of atoms - thinking with AI, voice as the test, the atomic substrate of linked notes. And it pulled up a LinkedIn post I had written in February: *How I Turn Scattered Ideas into Finished Writing (with AI)*. Roughly 1,100 words - the opening paragraph began *"I just got back from a week in Iceland."* The closing paragraph said *"the thinking was always mine. The AI just gave it infrastructure."*

In between these lines, there was a description of the main elements of my method. Atoms. MOCs. A multi-step workflow. Two drafting paths converging at *"would I say this?"* Voice as the load-bearing test. The whole thing already articulated, in my voice, before I had any of the language I was about to introduce.

That is when it hit me:

> **Me >** *"The more I read that earlier post, the more I realise I've pretty much written this piece months ago - before I had any content plan at all. lets refactor it, fit it into the arc and fill the gaps in the existing thinking."*

The empty canvas was no longer empty. Something I had drawn months earlier got pulled in as the substrate. See [[vault-as-substrate]].

The plan I had tabbed out a few minutes earlier flipped into a different artefact: a gap analysis. Same six sections which now became a checklist of what the existing piece does not have that next Thursday's piece needs. During our back and forth discussions we agreed on seven gaps which the AI presented back to me as follows.

1. The method needs a proper name. The February piece described the practice in full but never named it [[chain-of-cognition|Chain of Cognition]].
2. The callback to last week's [[reheated-thinking|Reheated Thinking]] is missing. The February piece has no enemy named.
3. The arc-fit needs work. The February piece stands alone; this one sits inside a series.
4. This piece needs a teaser for next week's worked example.
5. The cooking metaphor needs reconciling. The February piece uses Iceland and geothermal heat. The series of pieces I am writing is anchored on cooking.
6. The workflow steps need remapping. The February piece has six steps; the underlying method has five phases.
7. The framing needs to be method-first, not tool-first. The February piece names Obsidian and Claude Code by paragraph four.

We worked through closing them as the session ran. Gap 5 was solved by dropping Iceland entirely -

> **Me >** *"I landed that metaphor well enough in the original piece. lets remove it from this one to make sure we are not being repetitive in how we present the key concepts."*

Gap 6 collapsed the six linear steps into the method's five phases - the further simplification came during the editing. Gap 7 traded vendor names for functional spec; the line you can read in last week's piece reads *"What matters is the capabilities: linked notes, single-idea granularity, fast traversal, readable by AI as plain text."*

Gap 1 was the trickiest. The naming itself ran into a clumsiness problem.

The first version of the opening pivot tried to do too much in one breath.

> **Max Original output >** *"AI can be used to deliver Augmented Cognition. The mechanism I use is Chain of Cognition."*

Two brand-new capitalised names within a sentence of each other. The twin *-cognitions* echo stumbled inside a phrase rather than carrying across paragraphs.

> **Me >** *"It is clumsy prose to say *augmented* twice. The category is Augmented Cognition. The method is Chain of Cognition. Said back-to-back, it sounds cumbersome."*

The AI proposed four framings to fix it. I picked the one that split the names across the piece. Lowercase verb phrase opens. Capitalised method name takes the naming moment alone. The brand upgrade to *Augmented Cognition* arrives a paragraph later, once the reader has already met the method. See [[naming-choreography]].

Once we expanded to include a few additional talking points, here is what shipped, in paragraph two:

> **Final Output >** *"AI can be used instead to deliver augmented cognition. Most of today's AI conversation is about agentic use ... which operates on the visible 20% of what Brian Madden calls the Cognitive Stack. **Augmented Cognition** is the other direction: the category of work that amplifies the invisible 80% ... I want to name it here: **Chain of Cognition**."*

Three appearances of *cognition*, three different jobs. Lowercase *augmented cognition* in sentence one. Capitalised brand debut a hundred-odd words on. Method name takes the very last beat. The twin echo now runs as a rhyme across the paragraph rather than as a stumble inside a clause. This is a great example of how I choose to phrase something based on cadence.

By the end of Phase 1, the canvas held the substrate piece, the seven-gap analysis nearly closed, and a choreographed brand introduction that would carry into the published opening. The atom set was ready. Time to build the map.

## Phase 2: the map and the skeleton

Phase 2 takes what Phase 1 captured and shapes it. The atoms get arranged on a Map of Content. The skeleton gets proposed; the thinker disposes.

I asked the AI to write the Phase 2 section of the skeleton itself based on all the notes we created in phase 1.

> **Me >** *"What does Phase 2 look like in practice?"*

The first proposal came back as three generic bullets:

> **Max Original output >**
> *- The MOC is the staging home for the piece's atoms.*
> *- The AI proposes a skeleton, the thinker disposes.*
> *- The structure emerges from the network of atoms, not from a pre-decided outline.*

All true but extremely abstract. To fix this I redirected the AI to write it from what is happening *in this Phase 2*, right now, in this session. Admittedly a luxury I won't have in other pieces.

> **Me >** *"Use our phase 2 conversation now to expand what happens in phase 2. Real inception stuff, eh?"*

(Note the *eh?* I was talking to the AI via dictation, conversationally, like a person, and at moments like that I forget it isn't one. That rhythm keeps showing up.)

The AI rewrote nine bullets grounded in observed behaviour, covering topics which included:

- The MOC as a living document that keeps getting updated through later phases.
- Dispose-via-inline-edits.
- Multiple passes.
- Phase 2 surfacing Phase 1 gaps.
- Voice calibration starting at bullet level rather than waiting for prose.
- The exit test being *"stable"* rather than *"right"*.

That description carried into the shipped Phase 2. The reader of last week's piece walks through six concrete moves:

> **Final Output >** *"Rename sections where the AI's label is not quite right. Merge what it split; split what it merged; reorder; reword a bullet in your own phrasing. Drop bracketed comments where the move is not obvious."*

The descriptive verbs are the tell that the description was written from inside the act, not before it. See [[method-from-the-inside]].

The skeleton was now half-built. The new Phase 2 description had the thinker's judgement, taste, and narrative shape concentrating in this phase - atoms in, shape out. Reading that back on the page, I caught myself thinking of a question I had been asked on a podcast a few weeks earlier.

The question got put to me: if you build a second-brain vault using this method, what is to stop your employer hoovering off your cognition, getting rid of you, and keeping eighty percent of what you know at zero percent of the salary? My answer at the time focused on technical assurance - encryption keys, securing the context, reassurance for the person whose thinking is in there. A reasonable answer but an incomplete one.

Sitting in Phase 2 of the production, I was able to articulate the more complete answer.

> **Me >** *"I gave an incomplete answer in a podcast recently....."*

> **Me >** *"...the answer I should have given: the vault is the constituent atoms - the substrate of the thinking. Phase 2 is where the judgement, the taste, and the nuance for what story wants to be told all show up. That's the secret sauce. It is what makes the output more than the sum of its parts. It is what makes the thinker irreplaceable."*

The reason that answer arrived in Phase 2 specifically is that Phase 2 is the phase where it is true. The atoms are extractable. The shaping work is not. Four atoms got captured in the vault as I worked: [[the-secret-sauce-is-judgement]], [[the-vault-extraction-fallacy]], [[context-stack-in-chain-of-cognition]], [[personal-encryption-as-talent-market-signal]].

One sentence from this revelation carried into the close of Phase 2 in the shipped piece:

> **Final Output >** *"This phase is where your judgement concentrates. The atoms are raw material; the shape is your taste. An employer or an AI could extract the vault, but the vault is archive, not author."*

A new stub note was created for *the vault is archive, not author* where I can pick it up in future thinking sessions. It will be particularly useful when thinking about the value of the human in the loop.

Late in Phase 2, I read the skeleton end to end. Several paragraph-labels caught my eye. *Why this matters. Why it matters. The big claim.* Different sections all with the same construct - announcing what the paragraph was doing rather than letting the sentence carry the weight.

> **Me >** *"I dislike the 'Why this matters' construct. It is becoming prevalent in AI-authored prose. There are more elegant ways to show why something matters than flagging it with a label."*

The clearest worked example came from the Phase 3 section. The original bullet announced itself with a label:

> **Max Original output >** *The big claim: if the upstream thinking is solid, the quality of the final piece depends far less on who typed the first sentence than you would expect.*

> **Me >** *"Strip the label. Bold the sentence so it stands on its own. Same insight, show don't tell"*

> **Final Output >** *"If the upstream thinking is solid, the quality of the final piece depends far less on who typed the first sentence than you would expect."*

A new section was born in the MOC: Voice anti-patterns, sibling to Key voice signals. The rule got captured: drop labels that announce what the paragraph is doing. Let the sentence carry the weight. See [[announcing-paragraph-labels]].

By the end of Phase 2, the canvas held a five-section skeleton with the four phases nested, piece specific notes on voice anti-patterns, four new atoms in the vault, and the better answer to the podcast question already woven into the prose. The shape was visible. Time to write.

## Phase 3: draft and ruthless edit

Phase 3 turns the skeleton into prose.

Drafting top to bottom through last week's skeleton, every section needed some editing. The section that describes this process - turning AI prose into the writer's voice - needed more editing than any of them.

The first three paragraphs came back as abstract framing:

> **Original output >** *"This phase is where the stakes are visible on the page. In practice the moves live in a tight loop at the section level. The strongest signal of what sounds like you over time."*

I read them and they sounded nothing like me.

> **Me >** *"First few paragraphs sound nothing like me. There is irony in there somewhere."*

I rejected three Claude rewrite options and demanded simpler wording.

> **Me >** *"Be plainer. For example 'The next step is to generate a full draft from the skeleton'."*

The shipped opening of that section, after a rewrite that was about 20% AI and 80% me, reads:

> *"Phase 3 turns the skeleton into a full draft. There are two possible ways to do this. Both paths use the skeleton as a map. The difference is who expands the bullets into prose."*

Plain prose, action verbs, no abstract scaffolding. Three honest sentences and a setup for the two-paths bullet list.

On the fifth or sixth pass through the prose, I noticed our co-editing rhythm had not one but four distinct patterns. Three were predictable:

1. The AI proposes options; I accept the lean recommendation.
2. The AI proposes options; I pick against the lean.
3. The AI proposes "leave it alone"; I agree or disagree.

The fourth one surprised me. It came up during the rewrite of the tool-agnostic paragraph - the one resolving Gap 7 from the gap analysis. The AI's first attempt at it was AI-voicey methodology-spec language. I did not like it. Equally, I did not like my own articulation of why I did not like it but we arrived at a good result somewhere between the two points of view. This quote is from the immediate aftermath where I realised we arrived in a good place:

> **Me >** *"Here is a case where I don't like the direction you are going in. I don't even really like the direction my own feedback is going in. But you can use my poorly-worded direction as a centre of gravity to drag your direction more in line with the direction I want it to go in. God, that is a lot of directions. You get the point."*

The common assumption about working with AI is that the human needs to articulate what they want precisely. The fourth pattern says the opposite. Imperfect feedback is sufficient because the AI can triangulate direction from vectors. The thinker does not need to name the destination - just provide enough pressure for the AI to narrow the space. See [[imperfect-feedback-as-centre-of-gravity]].

The line that came out the other end

> **Final Output >** *"What matters is the capabilities: linked notes, single-idea granularity, fast traversal, readable by AI as plain text"*

is in paragraph nine of last week's piece. Imperfect-feedback triangulation produced it.

The shipped Phase 3 carries the multi-option discipline as a paragraph the reader can see. The four patterns I identified are the working layer this vignette adds. See [[four-co-editing-patterns]].

There is another axis underneath these patterns: who does the actual rewriting once the call is made. Sometimes I give the AI feedback and it rewrites. Sometimes I just rewrite the line myself. Sometimes both - the AI gets it closer, then I tweak the rest. The mix is what makes the prose mine. See [[two-axes-of-co-editing]].

Two small details closed Phase 3.

First, a word in the AI's draft of last week's Phase 2 section stopped me. Transcluded. I had no clue what it meant, so I asked. The AI explained (PKM jargon for embedding one note inside another by reference), then surfaced its own concern - the word leaned tool-specific, against the tool-agnostic neutrality principle I had set for the piece. It offered plainer substitutes. With the explanation in hand, the word actually fit the moment.

> **Me >** *"Leave it in, especially if it is relevant to PKM. I learn something every day. Maybe somebody will Google it and figure out how smart I am. And yes - I am fully aware that in the next article, when I show how the sausage was made, I will probably surface the fact that I had no clue what that word meant but we decided it was appropriate after closer inspection."*

The author of the piece on Chain of Cognition just told you he Googled a word from his own draft. Transcluded survived in the shipped Phase 2:

> **Final Output >** *"..the raw input log, transcluded or pasted verbatim...."*

And yet, in my view its inclusion in the final result was purposeful and shows that the method is working and authorship remains mine.

Second, a rendering bug. A heading was breaking. The AI went down a number of incorrect rabbit holes; I spotted it visually - a placeholder typo two paragraphs up, silently breaking everything downstream. The kind of diagnostic the AI is normally first to catch. See [[systematic-and-holistic-diagnosis]].

> **Me >** *"Max 995, Dave 1."*

> **Max >** *"&lt;sigh&gt;: And I just know this example of your greatness will make it into the next article."*

P.S. The above response caused the issue again this week - note to self angled brackets don't play nice in markdown. The wheel keeps on turning....

By the end of Phase 3, the canvas was filled out and I had an article where every idea was mine, every sentence was either accepted, tweaked or written by me and sounded like me.

## The Result?

One more thing got built into the shipped piece that did not come from the gap analysis: the method description itself simplified.

The underlying method I started with has five phases. Reading the skeleton through, two felt like they should be one. Phase 3: voice-calibrated draft and Phase 4: ruthless edit were doing the same work in tight loop at the section level. Splitting them imposed a sequence that does not match how drafting actually works. They merged into a single Phase 3.

A second simplification came at the very end. Phase 5: package described how to publicly stage the chain alongside the piece - the atoms, the MOC, the production log, all the trail. The section was substantial. It also did not belong here - it was its own argument and deserved its own piece. Out it came, into the backlog as a future article in the series.

Five phases became three. Phase 1 cyclical. Phase 2 the map and the skeleton. Phase 3 a merged draft-and-edit. The reader of last week's piece walks those three. The Package phase will get its own piece when the series arrives there.

I told you in the opening that the build below produces the lines you have already read. Here is the receipt. See [[the-build-produces-the-lines]].

The opening paragraph of last week's piece begins:

> **Final Output >** *"Last Thursday I named the enemy: **Reheated Thinking**. Opening the freezer, microwaving whatever came out, serving it to your guests, and calling it cooking. Swap the kitchen for the keyboard, and this becomes AI slop where someone types a short prompt, accepts whatever comes back, and ships it as their own thought. AI does not have to be that."*

That paragraph exists because the chef's-kitchen scaffold from the original plan got abandoned in Phase 1 in favour of refactoring the February piece. The metaphor swap (kitchen → keyboard) carried over from two weeks ago. *AI slop* earned its place by being plain enough to survive the Phase 3 voice tests.

Paragraph two carries the naming choreography. Lowercase *augmented cognition* in sentence one. *Augmented Cognition* a hundred-odd words on. *Chain of Cognition* on the very last beat. Three appearances, three jobs. The brand-upgrade ladder was the resolution to Gap 1 of the gap analysis.

Phase 2 of the shipped piece closes on:

> **Final Output >** *"This phase is where your judgement concentrates. The atoms are raw material; the shape is your taste. An employer or an AI could extract the vault, but the vault is archive, not author."*

That sentence exists because a podcast question resurfaced unprompted in Phase 2 of the production.

Phase 3 opens with:

> **Final Output >** *"Phase 3 turns the skeleton into a full draft. There are two possible ways to do this."*

Plain, declarative, action-first. That paragraph exists because the AI's first attempt sounded nothing like me, and the section about turning AI prose into the writer's voice ended up needing the heaviest editing in the piece.

Phase 3 closes on:

> **Final Output >** *"If the upstream thinking is solid, the quality of the final piece depends far less on who typed the first sentence than you would expect."*

That sentence exists because the original version had a *"The big claim:"* label announcing it - the AI-voice tell I caught in Phase 2 and listed in the voice anti-patterns. Strip the label. Bold the sentence so it stands on its own.

The closing thesis:

> **Final Output >** *"Atoms, MOCs, and outputs make a chain of cognition. Every link is yours."*

That line ended last week's piece as theory. This piece ends on it as evidence.

The chain is mine because every link was mine. The AI proposed, recorded, drafted, diagnosed. I, as the thinker, chose, redirected, taste-tested, re-wrote, kept or cut.
