Launching AI has been like launching a rocket. The first stage requires enormous thrust just to get off the ground: hundreds of billions in investment, breakthroughs in model architecture, entirely new infrastructure. Expensive, noisy, and from a distance it looks like a lot of fuel being burned for not much altitude.

The riskiest moment is the transition between stages. The first stage fuel is spent, the boosters are jettisoned, and for a brief moment the rocket coasts on momentum alone. If the second stage doesn't fire, the whole thing comes crashing back. That's the scenario the sceptics were betting on.

But the second stage is firing. The improvements in reasoning, the knowledge-work use cases, the SaaS repricing: that's evidence of thrust, not hot air.
