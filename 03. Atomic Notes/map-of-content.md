A Map of Content (MOC) is a bridging note I create when I notice five to ten atomic notes clustering around a single topic. It doesn't contain new thinking. It organises existing atoms into a chain of cognition: this idea leads to that one, which connects to this third, which contradicts that fourth. The narrative spine that turns scattered notes into an argument.

Every published article in my vault has a MOC. Each one shows which atoms make up the argument, plus a *Connecting Threads* section highlighting atoms that bridge into other articles. When the same atom appears in multiple MOCs, that's a load-bearing idea in my thinking - a thread worth pulling.

MOCs turn writing into assembly, not invention. By the time a MOC has a clear spine, the hard thinking is already done - it happened in the [[one-idea-per-note|atoms]] and the [[links-do-the-work|links between them]]. [[the-writing-is-a-byproduct|The writing is a byproduct]] of walking the map.
