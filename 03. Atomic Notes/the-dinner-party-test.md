The dinner party test is the diagnostic I use to tell whether a piece of thinking was cooked from scratch or reheated from a foundation model. Before publishing, sending, or presenting, I ask myself four questions:

- Can I name the ingredients - the sources, the evidence, the assumptions?
- Can I describe the method - why this structure, why this sequence, what I tried and rejected?
- Can I explain the substitutions - what I changed from a more obvious version, and why?
- If a guest asked "why this and not that?", could I answer from my own understanding rather than restating the dish?

If yes to all four, I cooked. If not, I reheated and plated.

The test is the practical defence against [[reheated-thinking|Reheated Thinking]]. It is the simplest version of [[chain-of-cognition|Chain of Cognition]] in operation: judgement applied to your own work before it leaves your kitchen. [[authorship-lives-in-judgement|Authorship lives in the judgement]] you applied along the way - the dinner party test is just where I check whether that judgement actually happened.
